The relay

The small service every request passes through on its way to the AI. The whole system lives inside it; the engineer never sees it.

The janitor

The relay's rule-following cleanup crew. At each checkpoint it trims, files the "minutes," leaves tombstones — by fixed rules, never by rewriting.

The advisor

The relay's background question to the same AI: "which blocks are you finished with?" Judgment for the janitor to execute.

Why this works

Deleting words is not deleting knowledge

Cutting 80% of a conversation sounds reckless — until you look at what the words actually are. A coding session is a thin thread of decisions wrapped in a huge amount of scaffolding, and the method is built to keep the thread and shed the wrap.

1 · The signal is thin

Most words did their job already

A 240-line file dump was needed for the ten seconds it took to find one function. The log of an error that's since been fixed is pure history. What actually steers the assistant — the task, the decisions, what was tried, where things stand — is a small fraction of the transcript, and that's the part we never touch.

2 · Verbatim or visible

Nothing is ever retold from memory

Everything kept is kept word-for-word — instructions, recent work, and important-looking lines (error messages, version numbers, decisions) that survive trimming on purpose. Everything removed says so, in place. Since nothing is paraphrased, nothing can be misremembered. That's the failure that kills summarize-it-with-AI approaches, and it's designed out.

3 · The repo is the real memory

The conversation is a window, not the warehouse

The code lives on disk. Test results can be regenerated in seconds. The transcript is just a working view of things that still exist — so removing a stale file-read costs, at worst, one cheap re-read. We're not burning the library; we're returning books it already finished.

4 · Meaning decides, not age

The author flags what's disposable

Rules alone judge by age and size — crude. The advisor asks the one party that understands the task, the AI itself, to name the blocks it's finished with; the janitor executes exactly that list and nothing more. Judgment chooses, rules enforce — each doing the job the other can't.

Trade-off 1 · size vs. frequency

The budget is a dial

Tighter budgets mean shorter (cheaper) messages but more cleanups — and every cleanup is one full-price turn plus a fresh chance to trim something needed.

Trade-off 2 · rules vs. judgment

Rules can't read minds

Fixed rules never misquote anything, but they judge by age and size, not meaning. Asking the AI itself what's disposable adds judgment — at the price of trusting it.

Trade-off 3 · deleting vs. tombstoning

Nothing vanishes silently

Removed blocks leave a one-line marker saying what was removed and why, and the original stays in the relay's archive. A wrong removal costs a re-read, not a disaster.
